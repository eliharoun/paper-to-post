# Headline Style Guide — Catchy, Relatable, and Honest

How to write the **title-card headline** (and the caption hook) so a curious professional
*stops scrolling* — without breaking a single anti-hype rule. This distills a four-lens
study of headline craft: professional tech/science journalism (Quanta, Ars Technica, The
Verge, MIT Tech Review, IEEE Spectrum, Nature/New Scientist news), viral social formats
(Two Minute Papers, Veritasium, Kurzgesagt, Fireship, TLDR/The Neuron), the copywriting
canon (Ogilvy, Sugarman, Schwartz, Caples; Cialdini and Loewenstein on the psychology),
and the science-communication-integrity literature (Sumner et al. 2014, *BMJ*).

**Read this alongside [instagram-writing-guide.md](./instagram-writing-guide.md).** That
guide owns the cards, caption, register, and the banned-hype list. This guide owns one
thing: **making the headline land.** Everything here operates *inside* those guardrails —
the validator still gates the post.

---

## The one idea

> **A headline's only job is to buy the swipe.** It is not a summary — it is a transaction
> that spends a thumb-second to purchase the reader's attention for card 2.

And the corollary that makes this pipeline different from clickbait:

> **The honest version is almost always the punchier version.** A specific true claim
> ("316× more per solved problem", "in mice", "top 10% on a practice exam") out-hooks a
> vague grand one ("AI replaces experts", "cure found"). Specificity, real scale, and
> genuine surprise are our catchiness engines. **The qualifier is the hook, not the tax.**

We are, in effect, the press release. Sumner et al. found downstream news exaggerates ~80%
of the time when the release does, and rarely when it doesn't. **The distortion starts or
stops at our headline.**

## The 7 techniques (amplify the real finding — never inflate it)

Each makes a true result hit harder. They're *orthogonal* to accuracy — you can max
catchiness and honesty at once.

1. **Specific number as the hook.** One concrete figure, front-loaded, beats any adjective.
   Precision reads as evidence; round numbers read as estimates; adjectives read as hype.
   Contrast two numbers for instant drama. → *"The best coding LLM cost 316× more per solved
   problem than the cheapest."*
2. **Tension / reversal.** Put two things that shouldn't coexist in collision, or overturn
   the reader's default assumption — *when the paper genuinely does.* → *"Flaky-test detectors
   ace the benchmarks, then fail on real code."*
3. **Concrete image / familiar analogy.** Map the unknown onto something the reader can
   picture, flagged as analogy ("like", "basically", "a kind of"). The analogy must be
   *structurally* true. → *"Give the AI a library card"* for retrieval-augmented generation.
4. **Human / practitioner stakes.** Answer "so what — for someone like me?" using stakes the
   paper actually bears on. Hedge downstream implications with *could / may / points to*.
   → *"…a shortcut that could cut inference cost."*
5. **Honest curiosity gap.** Open a specific question and *withhold only the how/why*, never
   the whether. **You must be able to finish "…and the paper's answer is ___" truthfully.**
   The "one" trick teases without overclaiming: *one gene, a surprising culprit, the
   unexpected trigger.*
6. **The "wait, really?" fact.** Lead with the single strangest *true* detail, stated flat.
   Deadpan only works if the fact is exactly true — don't round "sometimes" up to "always."
7. **Strong verb, scaled to the evidence.** A vivid verb creates motion; the *right* verb
   also encodes how strong the claim is (see the gradient below).

## The verb credibility gradient (pick the strongest verb the evidence earns)

```
prove ▸ show ▸ find ▸ boost/cut/beat ▸ suggest ▸ point to ▸ hint ▸ could/may ▸ linked to
 strongest claim ───────────────────────────────────────────────▸ weakest / safest
```

- **Single study?** Use *suggests / hints / points to* — never *proves / confirms*.
- **Observational / cohort / correlational?** Use *linked to / tied to / tracks with / comes
  with / associated with* — never *causes / prevents / boosts / triggers*.
- **Downstream implication the paper hasn't delivered?** Hedge with *could / may*.
- Punchy honest hedges that *add* intrigue rather than subtract confidence: **hints, points
  to, early sign, first look, proof of concept, a clue that.**

## High-performing verbs (all defensible)

discover · reveal · uncover · build · engineer · map · crack · solve · rewrite · overturn ·
upend · redraw · break · shatter · rethink · blur · points to · edges toward · closes in on ·
hunts · races · explains · emerges · hints · suggests · decodes · untangles · illuminates ·
beats · slashes · cuts · shrinks · restores

## Headline patterns (skeletons that travel)

Rotate them — a feed of one pattern trains the audience to scroll past.

| Pattern | Skeleton | Example (honest) |
|---|---|---|
| **Concrete reveal** | `[Vivid noun] [surprising verb] [the idea]` | "One video generator, retooled to see depth, pose, and objects" |
| **Tension / paradox** | `[Expected] but [violation]` | "Detectors ace the benchmarks, then fail on real code" |
| **Number hook** | `[Specific number] [that reframes scale]` | "The best coding LLM cost 316× more per solved problem" |
| **Reversal / myth-bust** | `You'd expect X. This found not-X.` | "The bug reports that help AI agents aren't the ones that help humans" |
| **How / Why explainer** | `Why [subject] [does surprising thing]` | "Why on-device AI burns more energy talking than seeing" |
| **Stakes / so-what** | `[Finding] could [change domain]` | "A shortcut that could make brain-like control 8× cheaper to train" |
| **Familiar anchor** | `[Unknown] is basically a [familiar thing]` | "A memory for AI agents that forgets on purpose" |
| **Colon two-parter** | `[Subject]: [the twist]` | "Antidepressants and dementia: an association, not a verdict" |
| **Quiet understatement** | `[Wild true fact], stated flat` | "The universe is expanding too fast, and no one can explain it" |

## Opener frames

`How [X does surprising thing]` · `Why [X does surprising thing]` · `[Actor] [strong verb]
[concrete object]` · `This [thing] [violates expectation] — [and they did it]` · `[Number]
[unit that reframes scale]` · `The [familiar analogy] for [domain]` · `[Belief]? Not anymore`
· `Inside the hunt to [goal]` · `Meet the [surprising named thing]` · `Turns out [surprising
true thing]`

## Every word must be motivated (don't orphan a concept when you de-jargon)

De-jargoning is good — but when you strip a technical term, **keep enough context that every
remaining word still makes sense.** If a verb or metaphor only works *because* of the term you
removed, the reader is left with an unmotivated word and the headline reads as confusing or
even wrong.

- **The failure:** *"On-device AI burns more energy talking than looking."* Dropping
  "vision-language model" orphaned *looking* — nothing tells the reader this AI processes
  images, so "why would an AI be *looking*?" The metaphor lost its referent.
- **The fix:** name the *kind* of thing just enough to motivate the vivid word —
  *"AI that reads images…"*, *"a chatbot…"*, *"a protein-folding model…"* — so *looking*,
  *talking*, *folding* each have something to attach to.
- **The rule:** before finalizing, check each concrete verb/metaphor and ask *"does the
  headline give the reader a reason this word applies?"* If a word's meaning depends on a term
  you cut, either restore a plain-language version of that term or change the word. Simplify the
  vocabulary, never the **referent** that makes the sentence coherent.
- This is the flip side of "no acronyms on the title card": drop the *jargon*, keep the *subject*.

## First-card / thumbnail craft

The title card is a **thumbnail whose job is to earn the swipe** — a door, not a summary.

- **One idea, one tension, one emotional charge.** If it needs an "and", it's two headlines.
- **Front-load the surprising word** — the first ~3 words register mid-scroll. Lead with the
  anomaly or the number, not the setup ("Researchers at…", "A new study…").
- **Concrete noun + strong verb.** Nouns anchor; verbs move the thumb.
- **"You"-framing where honest.** Pull the abstract into the reader's lap ("…on your
  hardware") — but never force a false "you" onto a result that isn't about them.
- **No acronyms / method names / hedge-clutter on the title card.** Save "LLM", "RLHF",
  "diffusion" for the inner cards. Card 1 is for your smartest friend who isn't in the field.
- **Read-aloud test:** if you stumble saying it, or it doesn't make you think *"wait, what?"*
  or *"how?"*, it's a summary — rewrite it.

## Honesty firewall (this is what separates us from clickbait)

**Hide the how/why, never the whether. Open only loops you close by card 3.** The
science/tech audience punishes overclaiming harder than any other — a burned reader never
saves again. Run every headline through these before it ships (mirrors the writing guide's
rules and the validator):

- [ ] **Species:** non-human subjects → species is in the **subject** of the headline
      ("…in mice"), never a trailing parenthetical (parentheticals get cropped on repost).
- [ ] **Causation:** observational/cohort/correlational → verb is *linked / tied / tracks
      with / associated*, never *causes / prevents / boosts*.
- [ ] **Certainty:** single study → *suggests / hints / points to*, never *proves / confirms*.
- [ ] **Banned words:** no `cure`, `breakthrough`, `revolutionary`, `miracle`,
      `game-changer`, `proven`, `guaranteed`, `100%` (the validator rejects these).
- [ ] **Denominator:** any number keeps its sample size / absolute risk / baseline.
- [ ] **Benchmark:** AI result on a test → say "on [benchmark]", don't claim general ability.
- [ ] **Honest gap:** can you truthfully finish *"…and the paper's answer is ___"*? If not,
      the curiosity gap is dishonest — rewrite.
- [ ] **Scope:** every qualifier that makes the claim *true* (in a dish, in one trial, so
      far, on this schedule) is present.
- [ ] **Amplify, don't inflate:** the drama comes from a real finding made vivid, not from
      upgrading a small finding into a big one.

### Punchy-but-FALSE vs punchy-AND-true

| Punchy but FALSE | Punchy AND accurate |
|---|---|
| "AI passes the bar, ready to replace lawyers" | "AI scored in the top 10% on a practice bar exam" |
| "Gut bacteria cause depression" | "The gut bacteria that keep showing up alongside depression" |
| "This compound burns fat" | "The compound that revved up fat-burning — in mice" |
| "New AI runs 10× faster with zero downsides" | "A quarter the size, and it barely dented accuracy — small enough to run on a phone" |
| "Scientists cure Alzheimer's" | "An experimental drug slowed Alzheimer's decline by 27% in a trial" |

### The single test

> **Would the scientist who wrote the paper read this headline and nod?**
> If yes, it's catchy *and* credible. If they'd wince, it's clickbait — rewrite.

## Length & mechanics (hard constraints from the writing guide)

- **Title-card `heading` ≤ 70 characters** (validator-enforced). Aim for **4–10 words** so it
  reads as a thumbnail.
- One idea; active voice; numerals not words ("7", not "seven"); no decoding-required puns.
- **No em/en dashes** (`—` / `–`) in the headline or anywhere else — they read as AI-generated and
  the validator rejects them. Use a colon for a two-part headline (`Subject: the twist`), or a comma.
- The **caption's first line ≤ ~125 chars** is the hook before Instagram's "…more" fold — same
  craft applies, and it must still carry the honest finding (see writing-guide caption rules).
- Keep the source/venue in the title-card `footer`, never a preprint label.

## Worked before/after (the move each makes)

- *"Multimodal Test Item Parameter Prediction…"* → **"Can AI grade a test question before a
  student ever sees it?"** — question hook + stakes; drops the jargon.
- *"Quantization-aware 4-bit inference…"* → **"Which LLM shrinking trick actually holds up on
  your hardware?"** — "you"-framing + honest curiosity gap the cards close.
- *"Senolytic clearance improves function in aged murine models"* → **"A drug that clears
  'zombie' cells let old mice run like young ones"** — analogy + species in the subject.
- *"Association between antidepressant use and incident dementia in a prospective cohort"* →
  **"Antidepressant use was linked to higher dementia risk in 461,464 adults"** — number +
  correlation verb ("linked"), denominator kept.
